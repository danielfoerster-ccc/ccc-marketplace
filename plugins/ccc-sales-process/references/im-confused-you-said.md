# "I'm Confused. You Said…" — Message Templates

*Reference doc for the [[ccc-sales-process]] plugin. Source: [[Gap Selling — Keenan]] Ch. 12 + Ch. 13. Lens: [[The Gap (Keenan)]]. Recipe: [[I'm Confused You Said — Reframe (Keenan)]].*

## The four-word phrase

> **"I'm confused. You said…"**

(Variants: *"I was under the impression that…"*, *"I thought you were aiming for…"* — same function.)

The phrase is load-bearing because it positions *you* as the potentially-mistaken party, which gives the buyer room to either correct your understanding (forcing them to defend their new position) or update their move (resolving the contradiction). Without "I'm confused", the same content reads as a gotcha.

## When to deploy

- A prospect goes dark after committing to a next step
- A deal stalls without explanation
- An objection appears that contradicts a stated future state
- Price pushback ("too expensive", "not in the budget", "can't afford it")
- A decision criterion appears that conflicts with the stated future state
- A team or partner makes a decision that conflicts with a stated priority

## The structural template

```
I'm confused. You said [SPECIFIC PRIOR COMMITMENT, in their language].

[OPTIONALLY: quantify the cost of their current move against that commitment.]

[QUESTION that forces them to defend the contradiction.]

[SHUT UP — silence is part of the technique.]
```

## Templates by situation

### Going dark

> *"Hey [Name], I'm confused. You said you were tired of [stated current-state pain] and that this would help you hit [stated future-state outcome]. I haven't heard back since [date]. Has something changed? Did you find another path?"*

### Stalled deal

> *"I'm confused. You said every month without [the change] you lose [€X / Y%]. We're [N months] in. Are you OK with that, or has the number changed?"*

### General objection that conflicts with stated future state

> *"I'm confused. You said [stated priority]. And now you're saying [new objection-based priority]. Help me understand which one is winning?"*

### Price objection — "too expensive"

> *"I'm confused. This produces [stated future-state outcome, quantified] for [price]. How is [price] too much for [outcome value, ratio of €N return per €1 invested]?"*

Then **shut up**. Don't justify, don't defend, don't soften. Wait.

### Price objection — "not in the budget"

> *"I understand, but I'm confused. If you don't move forward this quarter, you'll lose [€X over Y months]. That's [€Z]. Are you prepared to lose that to stay inside this year's budget line — or is the question really about something else?"*

### Price objection — "can't afford it"

This is structurally different from the other two — affordability is real, not a budgeting choice. Test which one it actually is:

> *"To be sure I understand — when you say can't afford, do you mean it's not in this year's allocation, or there's no available capital at all? Different problems."*

If it's a real affordability issue, walk. Refer them on. Integrity earns the next referral.

### Decision-criterion misalignment (during discovery / pipeline)

> *"I'm confused. You said [stated priority] was the most important outcome. But [decision criterion they listed] won't move that needle. Did I misunderstand what you're trying to accomplish?"*

### Demanding unreasonable terms

> *"I get why you want [the demand]. But just because you want it doesn't mean we have to give it. We need to be working together — if you can't meet me halfway with [counter-ask], we're not right for each other."*

(This is the "you're not the customer's b*tch" frame, Ch. 12.)

## Tone

Confused, curious, slightly puzzled — never angry, never sarcastic, never triumphant. If you can't deliver it that way, don't use it. The tone is the technique.

## Anti-patterns

- **Inventing the prior commitment.** The technique only works if the commitment was actually made. Pulling something out of thin air destroys trust.
- **Stacking objections.** One contradiction per message. If there are three, address the most load-bearing one and let the others surface.
- **Filling the silence.** After delivering the question, **stop typing / stop talking**. Most contradictions resolve when the buyer is forced to fill the silence.
- **Using it on someone in genuine emotional distress.** This is a structural challenge, not appropriate for grief, panic, or trauma contexts.
