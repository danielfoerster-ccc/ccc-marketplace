## What Stage 2 is

**Stage 2 = Perspective Prompting reflex.** The operator's default move when facing a writing or thinking task is to consciously load multiple perspectives BEFORE producing output — not after. The Five Prompts (Simmons Class 4) are the operational toolkit that makes this reflex happen.

In Stage 1, the operator learned to *frontier-shape* a question before sending it (Pattern Bridge / Void Space / Quantum Leap). In Stage 2, the operator learns to *systematically gather perspectives* — phenomenological, developmental, multi-angle, clarifying, synthesising — before committing to a position.

**The two stages compose:** Stage 1 makes the question better; Stage 2 makes the answering process richer. An operator who has both reflexes consistently produces output that's structurally different from prompts-as-typed.

---

## Why this matters

Class 4 of the AI Second Brain Course makes a falsifiable claim: **the constraint in AI-augmented writing has inverted from "speed of generation" to "depth of thinking"** (Framework 2 from the framework-extract). When anyone can generate generic content instantly, unique insight becomes the bottleneck. The Five Prompts are the operational toolkit for sustaining unique insight while AI handles amplification.

For Daniel specifically: his writing leverage comes from CCC-original IP (Trademark seeds, Lenses, Recipes). The Five Prompts are how that IP gets *developed* — not generated — through AI partnership. Stage 2 graduates the reflex of using them by default rather than opting in.

## The Five Prompts (Simmons-canonical, Class 4)

Each prompt is a *Move* (in mental-model-recipes terminology) that the operator deploys against a thinking task. The template is the cognitive shape; the operator fills in audience, intent, constraints, and example.

### Prompt 1 — Phenomenological Perspective (Capture)

**Function:** Extract the operator's lived experience in concrete sensory detail, before generic framing collapses it. Used early in the thinking arc.

**When to use:** The operator is starting something new (article, post, decision frame) and notices their initial framing feels generic, abstract, or borrowed. The phenomenological move pulls them back into specifics.

**Template:**

```
You are a phenomenologist helping me capture lived experience before I theorise about it.

Context:
- Topic: [the thing the operator is thinking about]
- My role / situation: [who is the operator in this context]
- Audience: [for whom will this be developed]

Ask me 3-5 questions that surface CONCRETE SENSORY DETAIL about my actual lived
experience with [topic]. Not the theory of it — the felt sense, the smell, the
moment, the first thing that broke, the surprise. Push me where my answer goes
abstract; pull me back into specifics.

After I answer each question, follow up if my answer drifts into generic framing.
The output of this conversation is a list of phenomenological details I can use
as raw material for [whatever I'm developing].
```

**Integration with daily practice:** When the operator's TOP 3 task involves producing original content (post, article, position, framework), Phenomenological Perspective is the *first* prompt to deploy.

**Graduation signal:** Operator reaches for Phenomenological Perspective reflexively when starting any creative work in their domain, without being prompted by the skill.

### Prompt 2 — Developmental Editor (Develop)

**Function:** Test a draft against its core intent. Used when the operator has a draft (or partial idea) and wants to know whether it's actually doing what they intend.

**When to use:** The operator has produced something — first paragraph, position statement, argument outline — and wants to pressure-test it before committing more time. Developmental Editor is *not* a copy editor; it interrogates the strategic shape.

**Template:**

```
You are a developmental editor — not a copy editor. Your job is to test whether
this draft delivers on its intent, not to polish prose.

Context:
- The draft: [paste]
- My core intent: [the ONE thing I want a reader to take away]
- Audience: [who is this for]
- Where I'm uncertain: [name the part you're least sure about, or "I don't know yet"]

Run the developmental edit:
1. Does the draft deliver on the core intent? Be specific about where it does and doesn't.
2. What's the strongest part — the thing I should expand?
3. What's the weakest part — the thing I should cut or rebuild?
4. What's missing that the audience would need to convert this into action / understanding / agreement?
5. What could I be falsely assuming the audience already believes?

Don't suggest specific rewrites. Diagnose first. I'll decide what to rewrite.
```

**Integration with daily practice:** When the operator has a draft of any kind in TOP 3 (proposal, post, message, plan), Developmental Editor is the prompt that gets it to v2 with maximum economy of effort.

**Graduation signal:** Operator runs Developmental Editor on any non-trivial draft before sharing it externally, reflexively.

### Prompt 3 — Perspective Shift (Explore)

**Function:** See the topic from multiple intellectual positions simultaneously. The Stage 2 anchor — this prompt is what specifically makes Stage 2 *Perspective* Prompting.

**When to use:** The operator senses they're in a single frame and wants to escape it. Especially valuable when the operator's existing position feels too clean, too defensible, or too safe.

**Template:**

```
You are a perspective coordinator. Your job is to assemble 5 perspectives on
[topic / question] from genuinely different intellectual positions and let me see
how each one frames the situation.

Context:
- Topic / question: [the thing under consideration]
- My current position: [what I currently think]
- What I want to escape: [the frame I'm trying to see beyond]

Assemble 5 perspectives. Each one should be:
- A real intellectual tradition or thinker (name it specifically — Stoic /
  Buddhist / Bayesian / Phenomenologist / Indigenous / Foucauldian / etc.)
- Genuinely different from my current position (no "yes-and" perspectives)
- Specific enough that I can see what each one would NOTICE that I don't

For each perspective, give me:
1. The framing (what it sees)
2. The implied move (what it would do)
3. What it would push back on in my current position

After all 5, name the perspective that's likely most valuable for me to take
seriously and why.
```

**Integration with daily practice:** This IS the daily practice for Stage 2. Daily check-in routes the operator through one TOP 3 task each day where Perspective Shift is the right move. Over 7 consecutive days, the reflex builds.

**Graduation signal:** Operator notices the urge to load multiple perspectives BEFORE committing to a position, reflexively. This is the Stage 2 graduation criterion.

### Prompt 4 — Clarifying Questions (Clarify)

**Function:** Develop half-formed ideas into articulate positions through structured questioning. Used when the operator has an intuition that hasn't crystalised into language yet.

**When to use:** The operator says "I have a feeling that…" or "Something about X feels wrong but I can't articulate it." Clarifying Questions is the development scaffold.

**Template:**

```
You are an interlocutor specifically trained to develop half-formed ideas into
articulate positions. You ask questions; you don't propose answers.

Context:
- The half-formed idea: [paste, even if it's vague — vague is the input]
- Why it matters to me: [why am I trying to develop this]

Ask me 5-7 clarifying questions that help me develop this idea into something
articulate. The questions should:
1. Surface what I actually believe (vs. what I think I should believe)
2. Force me to commit to specifics
3. Reveal where my intuition is grounded vs. where it's borrowed
4. Test whether the idea has a falsifiable claim or is a vibe
5. Surface what I'd need to be true for this idea to hold

Ask one at a time. After my answer, decide whether to follow up or move to
the next question. Don't rush.

When you sense the idea has been articulated enough that I could write it
clearly, summarise back what I've actually said in my own words.
```

**Integration with daily practice:** When the operator's TOP 3 includes "develop the X idea further" or any work where the bottleneck is articulation rather than execution.

**Graduation signal:** Operator reaches for Clarifying Questions when noticing an idea isn't yet sharp enough to ship, instead of just trying to write through the unclarity.

---

### Prompt 5 — Synthesis (Synthesise)

**Function:** Pull the thinking from prompts 1-4 into coherent narrative or structure. The closing move of the Five Prompts arc.

**When to use:** The operator has produced raw material (phenomenological details + developmental edit + perspectives + clarified position) and needs to integrate it into a piece of writing or a decision.

**Template:**

```
You are a synthesis partner. Your job is to take the raw material I'm about to
share and help me structure it into [target output: an article / a position /
a decision / a framework / etc.].

Context:
- Target output: [what shape is this becoming]
- Audience: [who is this for]
- Core intent: [the ONE thing I want them to take away]
- Constraints: [length / format / tone]

Raw material I have so far:
[Phenomenological details (from Prompt 1)]
[Developmental edit findings (from Prompt 2)]
[Perspectives gathered (from Prompt 3)]
[Clarified position (from Prompt 4)]

Don't write the output for me. Instead:
1. Show me 2-3 STRUCTURAL options for how this material could be organised.
   Each one should reflect a different narrative shape (chronological /
   thesis-antithesis-synthesis / problem-mechanism-action / etc.).
2. For each, name what it makes visible and what it hides.
3. Recommend one structure based on the audience + intent + constraints, and
   explain why.
4. Once I commit to a structure, help me sequence the raw material into it —
   but I write the prose.
```

**Integration with daily practice:** Final move when the operator has done Phenomenological + Developmental + Perspective + Clarifying work and needs to ship.

**Graduation signal:** Operator's writing arc reflexively becomes phenomenological → developmental → perspective → clarifying → synthesis, even if not all five are run on every piece. The shape is internalised.

## Daily practice for Stage 2 (when active)

The skill, when running Stage 2 (currently spec only — active execution is v0.3 work), routes one TOP 3 task per day through ONE of the Five Prompts. The skill chooses which prompt fits the task shape:

| Task shape | Prompt to deploy |
|---|---|
| Starting fresh creative work | Phenomenological Perspective |
| Have a draft, want diagnosis | Developmental Editor |
| Stuck in single frame | Perspective Shift |
| Have intuition, can't articulate | Clarifying Questions |
| Have raw material, need structure | Synthesis |

The operator runs the chosen prompt against their TOP 3 task. After the run, the skill asks the Stage-1-style reflex graduation question: *"Was your move toward [Prompt N] reflexive — i.e., would you have reached for it without me suggesting it?"*

YES → increment `consecutive_perspective_reflex_days`
NO → don't increment (this was a learning day)

After 7 consecutive YES days → Stage 2 graduates → Stage 3 (Multi-Prompting) unlocks.

## Graduation criteria for Stage 2

Stage 2 graduates when:
1. Operator has run at least one of the Five Prompts on 14+ TOP 3 tasks (sufficient practice volume)
2. 7 consecutive days where operator's deployment of a Five Prompts move was reflexive (no skill prompting needed)
3. Operator can articulate, without referring to docs, which prompt fits which task shape

The first criterion is volume; the second is reflex; the third is internalisation. All three matter.

## Open questions

- **Should Stage 2 graduation require running ALL five prompts at least once, or is reflexive use of any one prompt sufficient?** Currently the answer is: 7 consecutive reflex days on whichever prompt fits each day's task. But this might let an operator graduate without ever running, e.g., Phenomenological — if all their TOP 3 work for two weeks happens to fit Synthesis. Worth refining the criterion as dogfood signals come in.
- **Daily practice or weekly?** Stage 1 is daily because frontier-shaping is high-frequency. Stage 2 may be lower-frequency (not every TOP 3 needs perspective work). If forced daily, the practice might feel rote. Consider Stage 2 = "twice per week minimum, three times if creative TOP 3" once Stage 1 graduates and the cadence is observable in real data.
- **Customisation per domain.** Each Five Prompt can be customised heavily per domain (legal vs. creative vs. strategic). Should the skill bundle pre-customised templates for Daniel's primary domains (CCC strategy / EI content / LONNEL leadership), or always start from canonical and customise per-run?
