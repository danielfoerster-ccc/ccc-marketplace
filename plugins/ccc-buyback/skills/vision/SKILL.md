# 10X Vision Skill

**Workflow: Dream Without Limits → Add Clarity → Four Elements → Output**

The purpose of buying back your time is not to have free time. It's to have time for work that actually matters — work that moves toward something worth building. This session defines what that something is.

Session time: ~45 minutes.

---

## Background: Why This Matters for Operators

Most operators are trapped in execution. They're so busy doing the work that they've forgotten — or never got clear on — what the work is for.

A 10X Vision does four things that can't be done any other way:
1. **Drives innovation** — when the goal is 10X bigger, you solve differently. 2X thinking optimizes; 10X thinking reinvents.
2. **Energizes others** — people don't follow processes, they follow vision. A clear, exciting destination is the best recruitment and retention tool you have.
3. **Crushes distractions** — when you know exactly where you're going, irrelevant opportunities stop looking like opportunities.
4. **Simplifies decisions** — "Does this get me to the vision?" becomes a 3-second filter for every choice.

Dan Sullivan's principle applies here: **10X is easier than 2X.** 2X requires you to do more of the same. 10X requires you to think completely differently — and that's where the real leverage lives.

## Phase 1 — Dream Without Limits (~15 min)

This phase has one rule: **no "how", only "what".** The moment the operator says "but how would I..." redirect them back to the dream. Reality-checking happens in Phase 2.

**Q1 — The base question**
"If nothing was impossible — no limits on money, connections, technology, or time — what would you build? Not what you think you should build. What would you actually build if you knew you couldn't fail?"

Listen without interrupting. Let the dream unfold. Then probe:
- "What would the business look like at its peak?"
- "What problem would you be solving for people?"
- "What would your role be — day to day?"
- "What would your life outside the business look like?"

**Q2 — The 200-year question**
"What could you do that would be remembered 200–300 years from now? Not because it has to be that big — but because it forces you out of 2X thinking."

This question unlocks ideas that feel embarrassing to say out loud. Those are usually the right ones.

**Q3 — The scale question**
"If you kept doing what you're doing now, but 10 times bigger — same approach, same model, just 10X the scale — what would that look like? And is that actually what you want, or do you want something different?"

This often surfaces whether the operator wants to scale what they have or build something new.

---

## Phase 2 — Create a Clear 10X Vision (~25 min)

"When you can describe your future with the same level of detail as your present, you'll have a compelling vision."

The goal of Phase 2 is specificity. Take what emerged in Phase 1 and add dates, numbers, names, places. Make it vivid enough that you could paint it.

Work through the four elements one at a time:

### Element 1: Team

"To accomplish this dream, who will be in the room with you? Who needs to be on your team — what roles, what kinds of people? If you had a board of directors, who's on it? Be specific: name types of people, their skills, where they are in the world."

Push for specificity: not "a great marketing person" but "a CMO-level operator who's scaled a B2B brand to €5M, based in Europe, with a network in the agency space."

### Element 2: One Business

"Before the empire, there's one business. Which one — ABS, CCC, FULCRUM, or something new — is the vehicle that gets you there? What does that business look like at its peak? Revenue, team, offer, clients, delivery model. Be concrete."

Remind them: every major builder focused on one thing first. Steve Jobs had Apple. Arianna Huffington had the Huffington Post. Focus precedes empire.

### Element 3: Empire

"Now zoom out — after the one business is running, what does the empire look like? Multiple companies? Investment portfolio? Media? Philanthropic arm? What's the overarching structure, and what's the purpose that connects it all?"

### Element 4: Lifestyle

"Step into this future — it's 10 years from now and you've built it. What does a Tuesday look like? Where do you live? What do you do outside of work? Who are you with? What are you training for, travelling to, creating? What does money in the bank feel like? How old are you and what does that version of you feel like?"

Don't rush this element. The lifestyle picture is what makes the vision motivating — not just the business metrics.

---

## Phase 3 — Reality Check (~5 min)

**Q4**
"Looking at what you've just described: does this feel *too big to be real* or *small enough that it's not exciting*? If it's not a little uncomfortable, we may need to go bigger. If it feels completely detached from anything real, we may need to add more grounding."

Calibrate accordingly. The target: "this feels almost impossible but I can't stop thinking about it."

**Q5**
"What's the earliest checkpoint on the way to this? If the 10X Vision is 10 years out — what does the 3-year version look like? Just one sentence per element."

This creates the bridge to the Preloaded Year session.

## Rules

- Phase 1 must come before Phase 2. Never jump straight to the four elements. The unlimited dreaming in Phase 1 surfaces things that structured questions don't.
- Never let the operator reality-check during Phase 1. Redirect every "but how would I" back to the dream.
- Push for specificity in Phase 2. Vague vision is useless. "A great team" tells you nothing. "3 people: an operator, a content creator, and a closer, all remote, all in the €60k–€90k range" tells you what to hire for next.
- The Lifestyle element is the most important motivationally and the most skipped. Don't rush it.
- If the operator has a 3-Year Vision in their sprint file, use it as input for Phase 2 — but make them expand it. Sprint files tend to have short, functional goals. The 10X Vision should be visceral and specific.
- Keep all output in English.
- Never tell the operator their vision is too big. That's not your call.
